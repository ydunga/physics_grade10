Electric potential

Now that you know what a circuit is and that electric current flows through the circuit, you might ask what exactly is it that causes the electron to flow from one end of the circuit to the other?

To answer this very important question we first need to learn a bit about electric fields.

Until now, you've been told that objects with a similar charge repel each other, while objects with opposite charges attract each other.

An electric field is a related concept to this observation.

Now if you had an area where you had a lot of electrons together then this region would have a strongly negative electric field. It would also have the potential to attract positively charged particles.

Now in a battery, due to the chemical reactions inside, there is a collection of electrons on the negative terminal.

This creates a potential difference across the two terminals.

When the two terminals of the cell are then connected to each other via a circuit, the electrons in the negative terminal have a conduit through which they can flow to the positive terminal, which has a lower concentration of electrons (is more positive).

The electrons move from the negative to the positive terminal, but the arrows indicate the direction of travel of a positively charged particle. So if there was a positive charge, then it would move from the positive terminal to the negative terminal.

We can thus define electric potential difference as the work done to move unit of charge from one point to another.

The unit of measurement of electric potential difference is a volt (V).

What do you think 1 volt is equal to?

The two variables of interest when measuring electric potential difference are - work done and charge. The SI unit of work is Joules and the SI unit of charge is coulom (C).

So one volt is the potential difference between two points when 1 joule of work is done to move 1 coulomb from one point to another.

We can measure potential difference using a voltmeter.

Let's work through an example now.
