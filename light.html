Light and reflection

You've previously learnt that light is a transverse wave.

Transverse waves are waves in which the particles of the medium travel at a right angle to the direction of propogation.

Below you can see an illustration of the propogation of a transverse wave.

Sound on the other hand is a longitudnal wave.

You've also learnt about reflection of light from reflective surfaces.

The angle at which light hits a reflective surface is called the angle of incidence and the angle at which a ray of light gets reflected from the surface is called the angle of reflection.

The angle of incidence = angle of reflection

In this section you will explore how light is reflected on curved surfaces - specifically on spherical surfaces.

Convex and concave mirrors

Below you can see what a concave and convex mirror look like.

The grey side of the mirrors are non reflective. So in a concave mirror, the reflective side is facing inwards. While in a convex mirror the reflective side is facing outwards.

On the mirror, the spherical surface has a center which is called the pole (P).

If you were to imagine the curved surface of the mirror as a part of a full circle. Then the distance from the pole on the mirror to the center of the circle would the centre of curvature of the mirror.

For a concave mirror, the center of curvature lies in front of the mirror, whereas for the convex mirror the center of curvature lies behind the mirror.

The distance from the P to C is basically the radius of the cirlce and known as the radius of curvature (R).

The line that passes through the center of curvature and the pole is known as the principal axis.

If you look at the illustration below, you will notice that the rays of light hitting the convex and concave mirrors, get reflected and converge at a common point lying on the principal axis.

This point is known as the principal focus (F) of the mirror.

The distance between the pole and the principal focus is called the focal length (f).

The diameter(length) of the spherical mirror is called its aperture. For spherical mirrors with small apertures, the radius of curvature (R) is twice the focal length.

R = 2f

Image formation

The image formed after reflection from a spherical mirror depends on the position of the object relative to P,F and C.

To figure out what kind of image will be formed on reflection you can draw three rays from the object to the mirror.

The first ray has to be parallel to the principal axis. After reflection this ray has to pass through the principal focus.

The second ray has to pass through the pole of the mirror. After reflection this ray will be reflected back along the same path.

The third ray has to pass through the principal focus of the mirror. After reflection this will emerge parallel to the principal axis.

The point of intersection of these rays will tell you the position and size of the reflected image.

Below you can see an example of reflection of an object in a convex mirror. The object in question is placed at a distance larger than 2f.

The reflection of this object will be erect, smaller than the object and it will be a virtual image.

Whereas in a concave mirror, where the object has been placed right at C. A real image of the same size, but inverted will be formed.
