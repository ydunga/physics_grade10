Lens

The magnifying glass we showed you on the previous page actually uses a double convex lens, not a convex mirror.

A lens is nothing but a combination of two spherical mirrors.

A combination of two convex spherical mirrors is called a double convex lens. Our eyes actually have convex lens in them as well!

A convex lens is slightly thicker from the middle, while a concave lens is thicker on the edges.

The varying thickness is what makes the lenses unique.

Refraction

Until now, we've spoken about rays of light getting reflected from a reflective surface.

We've been studying how light behaves when it is travelling through the medium of air.

But what happens when light travels through a different medium that is denser than air?

A lens made of glass, for example has higher density than air.

So what happens when light travels through this medium?

Light changes direction when it enter a medium of higher/lower optical density. This phenomenon is known as refraction of light.

When light enters a medium - such as glass- which has higher optical density than air, the ray of light will be refracted towards the normal. The normal ray is a perpendicular ray at the point the light enters from one medium to another.

You can see in the illustration above two paths for the ray of light entering a glass slab. In one instace the light does not get deflected and enters and exists the glass slab unchanged.

In the second instance, the ray of light gets deflected towards the normal when it enters the glass and away from the normal when it exits the glass.

The ray that enters the glass pane is known as the incident ray and the ray that exits the glass after refraction is known as the refracted ray.

The refraction occurs because the speed of light changes when it moves from one medium to another.

Light travels fastest in a vaccume at a speed of 3 X 108ms-1. It travels at a slightly less speed in air, however the speed decreases considerably when it enters glass or water.

We can express the relative speed of light in a medium with respect to air.

This is known as the absolute refractive index of the medium.

The light that we see is actually made up of 7 colors. When you see a rainbow in the sky, you see the 7 constituent colors of white light because the light is getting refracted by the water droplets in the sky. We can create a similar effect by refracting light through a glass prism.

Refraction in spherical lenses

Similar to spherical mirrors, lenses also have a center called the center of curvature. Rays of light passing through the center do not get refracted.

Lenses also have a focal length, however they have two prinicpal focii instead of one.

We mentioned earlier that convex lenses are thick in the middle and thinner at the edges. How do you think light will be refracted in a double convex lens?

Convex lenses are converging lenses. They focus the rays of light passing through them at a particular point.

Concave lenses on the other hand diverge light rays passing through them.
