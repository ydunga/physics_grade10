Heating effect of electric current

In a closed circuit connected to a device, some part of the energy is used to power the device - light a bulb, rotate blades of a fan etc. Some residual energy might be given off in the form of heat energy.

As this heat energy is given off, the device with the circuit might experience a slow rise in its temperatire.

If you have a device where the electric device is purely resistive i.e a bunch of resistor connected to a battery then the current is converted into heat energy. This is how radiators, toaster and kettles generate heat!

In an electric kettle, you have a surface or coil which has high resistance. When electricity is provided to the kettle, the material with high resistance starts converting electricity to heat energy and warms up water!

This phenomenon is known as the heating effect of electric current.

So how much heat can we generate in this manner?

If you have a current I, flowing through a resistor of resistance R and the potential difference across the circuit is V.

Then the work done in moving a charge - Q across the circuit will be = V*Q.

The power source must be supplying V*Q/t amount of energy.

You know that Q/t is just the electric current. So the power being supplied is V*I.

If the power is being supplied over a period of time t, then the total power = V*I*t

This power/energy is what gets converted to heat energy and is released from the resistor.

We know from Ohm's law that V = I*R. We can plug that in to the equation above.

Which gives us H = I*R*I*t

H = I2R*t

This is known as Joule's law of heating.

Power

The SI unit for measuring power is Watt.

In earlier classes you had been told that 1 watt = 1 joule/ second.

We can also define one watt as the power consumed by a device that carries 1 ampere of current when operated at a potential difference of 1V.

P = V*I

P = I*R*I

P = I2R

OR P = V2/R

The commercial unit for measuring energy consumption in kiloWatt per hour. 1kWh = 3.6 X 106joules
