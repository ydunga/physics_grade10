Electromagnetic induction

In the previous section you studied the effect of a current carrying conductor being placed inside a magnetic field.

Imagine instead, a situation where a conductor is moving inside a magentic field or we have a conductor around which a magnetic field is changing.

Click on the magnet below to move it into the coil!


In the illustration above, if you move the magnet into the coil by clicking on it, you will see that the attached galvanometer shows a positive reading.

A galvanometer is an instrument that can detect the presence of a current in a circuit.

So why does the galvanometer show that there is current flowing the coil?

Let's take our experiment a bit further and maybe then we can answer this question.

In this second experiment, we have a conductor ring in the middle. Two coils of wire are attached to this conductor.

On the left hand side you have a circuit, where the switch is initially open.

On the right hand side the conductor is attached to a galvanometer to read if there is any current passing through the wire.

If you were to close the circuit on the left hand side (by clicking on the switch) then the galvanometer will show you a positive reading.

This means there is a current passing through the right hand coil even though it is not connected to a circuit!


This experiment was first conducted by Michael Faraday.

What is happening here is that a potential difference is induced in the right hand side coil whenever electric curent passes through the left hand coil.

This process by which a changing magnetic field in a conductor induces a current in another conductor is called electromagnetic induction.

Magnetic flux

To give you a better idea of what causes this induction, we will briefly talk about magnetic flux.

Magnetic flux is how any object (conductor or not) is affected by a magnetic field.

We had shown you earlier how we can visualise the magnetic field around a magnet using magnetic field lines.

So you can imagine the lines below showing you the magnetic field of a magnet.

Magnetic flux is determined by how strong the magnetic field is and how large the surface area of the object in question is. What we care about is the surface area that is perpendicular to the direction of the magnetic field.

Imagine we place an object in the middle of this magnetic field.

Now everytime, either the strength of the magnetic field changes or the position of the object changes, there will be a change in the magnetic flux.

You can see below that when we rotate the object, the number of magnetic field lines hitting the object is lower and the lines are no longer perpendicular to the object.

Now because the position of the object with respect to the magnetic field has changed, there will be a change in the magnetic flux as well.

And everytime there is a change in magnetic flux, we have electromagnetic induction which creates a current.

Now imagine if you could keep the object rotating constantly.

With this constantly moving object inside a magnetic field you could generate electric current!

Fleming's right hand rule

Just like we used Fleming's left hand rule to figure the direction in which force would be applied when a current carrying object is placed in a magnetic field, we can now use Fleming's right hand rule to find the direction of induced current when a moving object is placed in a magentic field.
