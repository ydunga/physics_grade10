Electric motor

To understand how an electric motor works we need to have a look at its structure.

First up we have a metal loop, known as an armature.

The armature is surrounded by two magnets, one acts as the north pole and the other acts as the south pole.

We could connect the armature to a circuit directly and it would turn into an electromagnet, however that is not of much use to us. A motor involves creating a rotating motion.

For this we need to connect the armature to a device known as a commutator.

The commutator being used here is like a hollow ring, with gaps on opposite sides.

The commutator is attached to the aramture and spins along with it.

To connect the armature+commutator to our circuit we add two brushes to each side of the commutator.

The brushes are usually made of metal shaped into a spring.

The brushes are then connected to the wires in the circuit.

The flow of the current in the circuit is shown below using the green circle.

When the current passes through the aramture(made of metal), the armature turns into an electromagnet.

Now when the current is passing in the direction of the black arrow shown below, we can use fleming's left hand rule to figure out the magnetic field of the magnets and the current will exert a force on the armature.

Click on the S/N shown in the coil to see what happens!

The armature will experience a force that makes it(and the commutator) rotate clockwise.

At this point- the commutator having also rotated will change the direction of the current. Which means the polarity of the electromagnetic aramture will flip.

Again, using Fleming's left hand rule we can figure out the direction of the force on the armature. This time the force will push the armature in a downward direction, thereby continuing the rotation.

This process repeats continously and keeps the rotation going.

So two things are happening simultaneously that keep the armature spinning.

First the flow of current through the armature turns it into an electromagnet and the current+magnetic field exerts a force to start the rotation.

Second, the commutator switches the direction of current every half rotation, which switches the polarity of the electromagnet and keeps the rotational movement going.

Isn't that neat?

Motors are used in many different kinds of appliances. From your refrigerator to a simple fan, all of these appliances work because of a motor!
