Electric generator

In an electric generator mechanical energy is used to rotate a conductor in a magnetic field, which in turn produces electricity.

The set up is very similar to what we had in the electric motor.

The main differences are that instead of a split ring commutator, we now have two slip rings connected to brushes.

and we have a shaft which inputs the mechanical energy and makes the conductor coil rotate.

You have to remember that in a motor, electric energy is converted into mechanical energy, while in a generator mechanical energy is converted into electric energy.

When the coil is rotated, the induced current will be generated. However with every half rotation, the direction of the current will change.

You can see below two scenarios.

In the first one the coil is moving up and the direction of the current is shown by the arrow.

After half a rotation, the coil will now move down. As shown in the second illustration. Now the direction of the current will reverse.

This kind of current, where the direction is changing over a specified time period is called an alternating current.

In the electric motor, we were able to get a rotation movement because the split ring would change the direction in which the current would flow through the circuit. But the current itself did not change direction. The flow of the current through the whole circuit was unidirectional. Such current is known as direct current.

Most power stations produce alternating current.

The mechanical energy needed to turn the coil can come from either running water, burning fossil fuels or nuclear reactions.

In hydroelectric plants, the potential energy of the water makes a huge turbine rotate, which in turn rotates the generator which produces electricity!

The rate at which alternating current reverses direction is called its frequency. In India the frequency of AC is 50Hz.
