Electricity

In grade 10 chemistry you were given an example of how portable batteries allow for electric current to power small electrical appliances.

The flow of electricity is nothing but moving electrons.

The closed path in which electrons flow is known as a circuit.

In our studies of electricity and the flow of electric current, we represent circuits using simplified diagrams, like the one shown below.

You might be wondering what do the different symbols in the circuit mean? Let's go through them one by one.

Components of a circuit







So the basic circuit shown to you above consists of a bulb, a cell, a switch and an ammeter.

What do the arrows in the circuit diagram represent?

Electrons, which are negatively charged- move from the negative terminal of the cell to the positive. In a circuit, the direction of electric current is taken as opposite to the direction of flow electrons.

So the arrows represent the direction of the flow of current.

How do we determine how much current is flowing through the circuit?

If a net charge Q flows across a conductor over a time period 't' , then we say that the current I = Q/t .

The unit of measurement of electric charge is coulomb (C).

One coulomb contains a charge equivalent to 6 X 1018 electrons.

Electric current - which is the flow of electrons is expressed in amperes(A).

One ampere consists of the flow of one coulomb of charge per second.

We can use the ammeter to measure the electric current flowing through a circuit.
