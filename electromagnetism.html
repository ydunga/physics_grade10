Magnetic effect of electric current

Before we get into the details of the magnetic effects of electric current, i want you to realise that you have already been told that a wire with an electric current running through it acts as a magnet!

We've been talking about how electrons (negatively charged), move towards the positive terminal of a battery in a closed circuit. That is in essence what a magnet is, isn't it? A magnet is something that can attract an oppositely charged particle or object!

Consider a bar magnet shown below. This will have a north pole and a south pole.

Just like we represented the "field" around a positively and negatively charged particle, we can show the magnetic field around a bar margent.

We can represent the "field" using lines, where the direction of travel is from the north pole to the south pole.

Another way to think about the magnetic field lines is that they trace the movement of a magnetic compass needle when it is placed near the two end of the magnet.

A magnetic field has both magntiude and direction. The direction is from north to south.

The magnitude or the relative strength of the field can shown by how close the magnetic field lines are to each other. As you can see from the illustrations above, the lines are closes together at the poles.

Now that you know a bit more about magnetic fields you can understand the magnetic field created around a current carrying conductor.

When an electric current is flowing through a wire, the magnetic field surrounding the wire can be represented by concentric circles as shown below.

The direction of the magnetic field will change as the position of the current carrying conductor changes.

To find the direction of the magnetic field you can make use of the right hand thumb rule.

In the right hand thumb rule you have to point the thumb of your right hand in the direction of the current (not the direction in which electrons are moving) and then the direction in which you wrap the rest of your fingers, is the direction in which the magnetic field circles are formed.

Magnetic field through a circular loop

If our wire, through which current is flowing is not a straight line and is a circular loop instead, how do you think this will affect the magnetic field surrounding the wire?

The rules for formation of magnetic fields still apply similarly to a circular wire.

The main point to note is that as we move away from the edges of the circle, towards the center, the concentric circles forming the magnetic field would grow larger and larger.

By the time we reach the middle of the circle, the arcs of the magnetic fields will simply appear as straight lines.

If you were to form a coil, by repeatedly forming circular hoops of a wire, the magnetic field produced by each circle will be additive. So we can amplify the magnetic field of a current passing through a wire by shaping it into a coil.

A solenoid is a coil of cylindrical shape formed by repeatedly shaping insulated copper wire into loops.

The magnetic field surrounding a solenoid is shown below. You will notice that the magentic field of a solenoid resembles that of a bar magnet!

This means one end of the solenoid works as a south pole and the other end works as a north pole.

By placing a magnetic material inside a solenoid, we can magnetise the object.

A magnet formed through such a process is known as an electromagnet.
